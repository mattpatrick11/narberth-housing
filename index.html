The Pattern: Of six major new construction communities in Ardmore, not a single unit is affordable for any household size at 80% AMI — even before factoring in utilities. Ardmore has more than 5× Narberth's rental inventory, yet its affordable stock comes almost entirely from buildings built between 1926 and 1975.

Finding 1: Narberth already has affordable rental housing across all household sizes — even when $150/month in utilities is factored into the affordability calculation. Montgomery Court alone satisfies every household size threshold.
Finding 2: Ardmore's new construction produced exclusively luxury units. When utilities are factored in, the tighter rent thresholds eliminate even the few new-construction units that previously margionally qualified. More inventory does not mean more affordable inventory.
The Risk: Luxury development raises land values, property taxes, and neighborhood character — threatening the existing older buildings where affordable units actually live. The proposed height bonus could displace the affordable housing Narberth already has.
  • Conduct a formal housing needs assessment before enacting zoning changes
  • Engage with existing affordable property owners on long-term affordability preservation
  • Consider targeted incentives (tax abatements, expedited permitting) rather than blanket height bonuses
  • Protect existing affordable stock from displacement driven by new luxury development
